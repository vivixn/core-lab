Flavourings  ––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––––

The six cloves of garlic that go into Lindsay Bareham's soup are a very welcome
addition, as far as I'm concerned – because they're tossed into the pan raw, they add
the kind of fiery heat which I associate with other typically Eastern European favourites,
like vodka and horseradish, both of which would make a fine accompaniment to the
finished dish. Maria Lemnis suggests black peppercorns, allspice and a bay leaf; a
mixture of hot and sweet flavours which seems appropriate; beetroot has such an
assertive flavour that it will take over the entire meal given half a chance, which I
think would be a shame. It should be the star here, but it needs some supporting actors
to point up its virtues.

Borscht should be a hearty, yet sophisticated dish: a bowlful of sweet, sour and
savoury flavours, rather than simply a vehicle for beetroot. It takes a bit of work –
but with a dollop of rich sour cream, and a sprig of aromatic dill, it's one of the
world's great soups. No wonder everyone wants to claim it as their own. Serve with
thick sour cream, sweet dill, and mushroom dumplings or hunks of black bread.
